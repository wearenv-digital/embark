When it comes to commercial property, property values are often higher than residential property values, resulting in greater financial risks and a more difficult decision-making process, making it especially important to know all the facts before you embark on your investment journey, and to have the advice of trusted commercial leasehold property experts.

If you are considering purchasing a property that is freehold, you own the property and the land it sits on outright. However, purchasing a leasehold commercial property means you own the building for a fixed period of time, meaning you are subject to a contract between yourself and the freehold property owner.

For many investors, the preferred option is usually a freehold of a commercial property. However, if you are looking to primarily use the property as a hub for your business, potentially a commercial leasehold investment may be more attractive. Points to consider when embarking on leasehold commercial property investment in the UK

Leasehold commercial property investment allows a flexible means of ownership. The lease can be negotiated with the landowner, helping you to create a commercial property investment plan that suits your business or investment needs. The leasehold of commercial properties has fewer upfront costs compared to the equivalent purchase of a freehold commercial property, allowing your business to scale more easily with less investment.

If you are anticipating that your business may need to expand in the near future, you may wish to consider a shorter-term lease. Additionally, as a leaseholder, you will bear less financial risk from the cost of expensive structural repairs to the property.

To maximise yield as a commercial leasehold investor, you must consider the below points. Our knowledgable team at Embark Development Group can advise. Positioning is one of the most important factors, as there is not much point in purchasing a leasehold commercial property with a 25+ year lease, if your business will struggle due to a substandard location choice or a rent that is crushing your company finances.

Length of lease

The length of the lease will impact your ability to get a mortgage. Lenders may need the lease to run 25-30 years after the end of your mortgage. Therefore, if you wish to obtain a 25-year mortgage, the lease must be 55 years before termination. It can be difficult to sell a property if the lease is less than 80 years.

 Maintenance

A benefit of leasing a commercial property is that you are not liable for the majority of maintenance costs, or they are included in the service charge you pay your landlord. Leasing can be an attractive option as you do not need to consider additional costs, which can be especially attractive if you are a scaling enterprise.

Location

Office spaces require close proximity to public transport networks and warehouse facilities need easy access by road. You should consider if the location is in an area of business growth or business decline, and compare the price of the commercial leasehold property to other premises locally.

Security Deposits

The leaseholder must pay a security deposit to the landowner which can be held for the entire duration of the lease contract. As with residential properties, damages not considered wear and tear, are likely to be redacted from your deposit. The remainder of the deposit is returned at the end of your lease period.

Ground Rent

The 1925 Law of Property Act dictates that ground rent must be paid for the land of your leasehold property, It is important to note that the commercial leasehold ground rent may increase during the duration of your tenure. If you have concerns regarding ground rent increases, speak to the Embark team today.

Service Charges

Service charges are standard industry practice and may include cleaning fees, waste disposal, the usage of energy, costs of security such as access control, CCTV and the costs associated with air conditioning and general building maintenance. Service charges are usually charged per square foot.
